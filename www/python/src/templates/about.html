About

This website was created by Tiago Lubiana in 2024. It is designed to provide an easy-to-navigate interface for exploring the Vickaryous & Hall cell classification system using data from Wikidata and Wikipedia.

In 2006, Matthew Vickaryous and Brian Hall published a comprehensive list of over 400 cell types found in humans based on previous lists and the literature in general (available at doi.org/10.1017/s1464793106007068). The article was published in the journal Biological Reviews of the Cambridge Philosophical Society, and it presents a philosophical and historical review, with a clear catalog.

I could not find a more recent catalog focusing on human cell types, especially one meant for humans to read. The Cell Ontology is probably the most used catalog of cell types technically, as it is used for data annotation (e.g., by CELLxGENE). However, it is a multispecies ontology without an obvious human subset AND it does not make the explicit assumptions that Vickaryous & Hall made.

These explicit assumptions include (list adapted to bullet point by T.L.):

"The catalogue was initially assembled assuming that all cell types recognised in the literature are natural entities (i.e., exist independent of investigation and investigator; Rodieck & Brening, 1983). In addition, this rationale draws attention to even poorly known and weakly supported cell types, with the expectation that future investigations will provide more definitive evidence supporting (or refuting) such claims (see also Rodieck & Brening, 1983). The composite data set was then modified as follows:

  1. Any cell types identified as stem or immature members of a particular cell type lineage were removed.
  2. Redundant cells (cells present on more than one list) were removed so that each cell type was represented only once.
  3. In situations where the single inclusive cell type of one (group of) author(s) represented several exclusive cell types of another (group of) author(s), the default was to accept the largest number of subdivisions as the actual number of cell types, except in situations that contradicted statement 1 (above).
For example, Lentz (1971) listed four different cell types corresponding to stages in the maturation series of an epithelial keratinocyte. Since these ‘ cell types ’ are deemed to represent immature members of the epithelial keratinocyte lineage, only one cell type (epithelial keratinocyte) is retained. Similarly, erythrocytes are a single cell type."

"Beginning with the cell as a component of one of the four basic tissue types (epithelial, nervous, muscular, and connective tissue; the ‘ special purpose ’ criterion of Mayr & Bock, 2002), subsequent decisions were based on histological/cytological characterisations commonly used to describe cells. These criteria included the relative position of a cell to other anatomical structures (e.g., covering or lining), function (e.g., support or blood/ immune), and biochemical product (e.g., mucous or sebaceous). Inherently, such decisions are fraught with subjectivity. In an attempt to remain conservative, in this analysis these decisions were made in consultation with traditional human histological texts (e.g., Rhodin, 1974; Bloom & Fawcett, 1975), as well as the different classes of cell types presented by Lentz (1971), Alberts et al. (1989), and Campbell et al. (1989)."

There is great merit in putting assumptions in the clear, and that is one of the reasons why their catalog is extremely useful. While I may have different opinions on what to count as cell types, the quality of their work allows me to build upon it in all clarity. I believe their classification should be studied in detail by anyone intending to classify human cell diversity.

For this website, I have curated links from the cell types listed by Vickaryous and Hall to their mapped entries on Wikidata. I've been curating cell types on Wikidata as part of my PhD. I mapped previously existing terms on Wikidata and created new terms where needed. Some Wikidata terms are linked to Wikipedia, which allows me to pull them directly into the page for each cell type. Please, feel free to contribute more information about the cell types on Wikidata and Wikipedia!

The site leverages the power of linked data and semantic web technologies to dynamically present information about various cell types and their hierarchical classifications.
